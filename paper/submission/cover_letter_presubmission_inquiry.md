# Pre-submission inquiry — Journal of Archaeological Method and Theory

**Subject**: Pre-submission inquiry — methodological framework for testing corridor-alignment claims

---

Dear Professor Michelaki and Professor Roux,

I am writing to inquire whether the *Journal of Archaeological Method and Theory* would consider a manuscript that introduces a portable statistical framework for evaluating claims of geographic corridor alignments among cultural sites — a methodology demonstrated on the canonical Michael ley line in southern Britain. The journal has a strong record of publishing quantitative methodological contributions, and the present work is intended to align with that tradition.

The motivation is methodological. Alignment claims of this general class (ley lines, sacred geographies, deliberate landscape spatial planning) have recurred in the archaeological literature for a century, and the principal statistical critiques (Forrest 1976; Williamson and Bellamy 1983; Johnson 2006) have established that naïve site-counting along an alignment is uninformative because of the high density of the British archaeological landscape. Despite this consensus, no standardised framework has, to my knowledge, been operationalised for testing future alignment claims on equal terms. The present work introduces a six-concentric-tests methodology that addresses each major confound — catalog curation, background density, orientation effects, multiple-catalog conjunction, definition sensitivity, and scale-of-claim — as a Monte Carlo procedure applied to a fixed bounding box.

As a worked example, the framework is applied to the canonical Michael ley line corridor. The data establish a regional population-level significance (joint conjunction p = 0.0002 at 50 km half-width, surviving Bonferroni correction across five widths and three orientation-control tolerances) while the global UNESCO sanity check explicitly rules out a planet-wide interpretation. The empirical claim is therefore narrow and the test is silent on mechanism.

All code, reference catalogs, and per-trial outputs are openly archived under DOI [10.5281/zenodo.20312153](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20312153) (Apache-2.0 and CC-BY-4.0). The manuscript is approximately 7,500 words with three figures and would be submitted as a regular research article.

**Abstract**

Claims that geographically dispersed cultural sites lie along corridors more tightly than chance — ley lines, sacred geographies, deliberate landscape alignments — have recurred in the archaeological and cultural-geographic literature for a century, but their statistical adjudication is complicated by three confounds operating simultaneously: catalog curation, background site density, and orientation effects from landmass directional structure. Prior critiques (Watkins 1925; Forrest 1976; Williamson and Bellamy 1983; Atkinson 1982; Johnson 2006) have addressed these confounds piecemeal but have not been operationalised as a portable test framework. We introduce a six-concentric-tests methodology in which a candidate corridor must survive an internal null, an isotropic population null on independent reference catalogs, a bearing-restricted null controlling for landmass orientation, a joint conjunction across multiple catalogs, a pole-definition sensitivity check, and a global sanity check distinguishing regional from planet-wide significance. As a worked example, we apply the framework to the canonical Michael ley line in southern Britain, using three independent reference catalogs derived from OpenStreetMap (754 St Michael place-of-worship records, 2422 strict prehistoric monuments, 5500 broad archaeological sites; bounding box 49.5–53.5° N, 6.5° W–2.5° E). The canonical corridor passes the four population-level tests at the 50 km half-width with joint conjunction p = 0.0002 (one-sided, 10,000 trials), surviving Bonferroni correction and remaining stable across three orientation tolerances and four independent corridor pole definitions. The global UNESCO sanity check does not reach significance, confirming the signal as regional. The result is statistical and geographic; the test is silent on mechanism.
