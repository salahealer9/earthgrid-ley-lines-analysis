# Abstract

Claims that geographically dispersed cultural sites lie along corridors more tightly than chance — ley lines, sacred geographies, deliberate landscape alignments — have recurred in the archaeological and cultural-geographic literature for a century, but their statistical adjudication is complicated by three confounds operating simultaneously: catalog curation, background site density, and orientation effects from landmass directional structure. Prior critiques (Watkins 1925; Forrest 1976; Williamson and Bellamy 1983; Atkinson 1982; Johnson 2006) have addressed these confounds piecemeal but have not been operationalized as a portable test framework. We introduce a six-concentric-tests methodology in which a candidate corridor must survive an internal null, an isotropic population null on independent reference catalogs, a bearing-restricted null controlling for landmass orientation, a joint conjunction across multiple catalogs, a pole-definition sensitivity check, and a global sanity check distinguishing regional from planet-wide significance. As a worked example, we apply the framework to the canonical Michael ley line in southern Britain, using three independent reference catalogs derived from OpenStreetMap (754 St Michael place-of-worship records, 2422 strict prehistoric monuments, 5500 broad archaeological sites; bounding box 49.5–53.5° N, 6.5° W–2.5° E). The canonical corridor passes the four population-level tests at the 50 km half-width with joint conjunction p = 0.0002 (one-sided, 10,000 trials), surviving Bonferroni correction and remaining stable across three orientation tolerances and four independent corridor pole definitions. The global UNESCO sanity check does not reach significance, confirming the signal as regional. The result is statistical and geographic; the test is silent on mechanism. The framework, code, and reference catalogs are open-source under Apache-2.0 / CC-BY-4.0 and archived at Zenodo (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20307501).
